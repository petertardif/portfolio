I read a lot of books that I liked in the moment and forgot by the next one. These three weren't like that — they changed a decision I actually made, more than once.

## The ones that stuck

**Extreme Ownership** — I read this during my administrator years, and it's the reason I stopped saying "the team dropped the ball" and started asking what I'd failed to make clear. Uncomfortable the first time. Useful every time after.

**The Score Takes Care of Itself** — Bill Walsh's book about standards over outcomes. I think about this every time I'm tempted to cut a corner because "it'll probably be fine." Standards are what you do when it's inconvenient, not when it's easy.

**Shoe Dog** — Less a leadership book, more a permission slip. Phil Knight's story is mostly about barely holding it together for a decade. It made me a lot more patient with my own timeline.

## Why these three

None of them are the obvious picks. But each one changed a specific habit — how I assign blame, how I set standards, how I judge my own pace — and that's the only test I use anymore for whether a book actually mattered.
