I'd been saying "this weekend" about this workbench for two years. Every time I needed a flat surface to work on, I improvised — a sawhorse and a door, the tailgate, the kitchen island (don't tell my wife). Eventually the improvising took longer than just building the thing would have.

## The plan

Nothing fancy: a torsion-box top, 2x4 legs, a shelf underneath for the stuff that always ends up on the floor. I drew it out on the back of a receipt, which in retrospect was a preview of how precise this whole project was going to be.

## What actually happened

The lumber yard didn't have the length I wanted, so the first hour was spent redesigning around what was actually in stock. Classic. Then my four-year-old appointed herself Head of Level-Holding, which slowed things down by about 40% and improved my mood by a lot more than that.

A few honest notes for next time:

- Pre-drill everything. I didn't, and split two legs I had to replace.
- Buy an extra sheet of plywood. You will mismeasure something.
- "Good enough" is a real finishing standard for a garage workbench. Let it go.

## What I'd do differently

Bigger shelf underneath, and I'd finish it before putting anything on top of it — I'm now working around a half-organized pegboard because the bench got claimed by clamps before I got to it.

Small win, but a real one. It's flat, it's sturdy, and it's mine.
